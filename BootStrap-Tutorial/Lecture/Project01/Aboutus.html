The samosa, despite being a popular Indian snack, actually originated in the Middle East. Specifically, it is believed to have originated in Persia (modern-day Iran) and Central Asia around the 10th century. The earliest mention of a dish similar to a samosa can be found in the writings of the Iranian historian Abolfazl Beyhaqi in his work "Tarikh-e Beyhaghi" where it was referred to as "sambosa". It was a small, meat-filled pastry, often carried by travelers and merchants.

In Somalia, during the holy month of Ramadan, the sale of samosas and other foods during daylight hours is banned to maintain the sanctity of fasting. The ban ensures that people respect the fasting period, and the sale of food during the day is viewed as a disruption to religious practices
